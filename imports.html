Exercise 11

Do concentration-driving imports map to exports?

Question
Import concentration is tied to export production chains.
Supports yes if
Concentration-driving imported intermediates are more export-linked than other concentration-driving imports.
Weakens yes if
They are less export-linked, or no more export-linked, than non-intermediates.
Current answer
Weakens the broad processing claim, but supports a narrower supplier-exposure channel. Product-Gini contribution is negatively linked to export value (-3.527) and export probability (-0.151), while supplier-country HHI contribution is positively linked to export value (0.075).

  • Energy has the sharpest within-bin Product Gini across HS6 energy products: median 0.887, median top-1 share 56.3%.
  • Intermediates are the largest import bucket: median import value share 48.0%.

Exercise 3: Import Bins

Energy has the strongest within-bin Product Gini across HS6 products and a positive leave-one-bin-out contribution. Intermediates matter more by scale: they are a large part of the import bill and include specialized input categories where a few HS6 lines can carry meaningful value.

Import value share means the bin's share of a country's total import value in a country-year. Product Gini within bin measures concentration across HS6 products inside that bin. Top-1 product share is the largest HS6 product's share of that bin. Leave-one-out Gini effect is the change in overall import Product Gini when the bin is removed; positive values mean the bin raises concentration.

      Exercise 4 and H2.4: Dominant Suppliers

      There are two different supplier-dominance concepts. Exercise 4 is country-specific: within an importer-country and HS6 product, it asks how much that country buys from its largest source. H2.4 is global: within an HS6 product market, it asks how much of world reported imports comes from the largest supplier country.

      In Exercise 4, top-supplier share is largest-source imports divided by one importer country's imports of that HS6 product. The interactive country-specific chart summarizes 33 reporter countries with 2025 data, and India in 2024 has top-supplier share at least 75% in 31.7% of imported HS6 rows. In H2.4, top-supplier share is largest supplier-country value divided by full-world reported imports of the HS6 product; in 2024, the median world product-market top-supplier share is 32.4%.

      How Much Partner Gini Comes from Within-Product Supplier Dominance?

      This is the country-centric version of the supplier question. For each importer-year, we first compute actual import Partner Gini after summing all HS6 products by source country, including HS6 999999 in partner totals. Then we rebuild the same importer-year after spreading each identified product equally across its observed suppliers, with HS6 999999 held fixed because it is not a real product identity. Across latest available country-years, median actual Partner Gini is 0.900; the conservative counterfactual median is 0.725. The median reduction is 0.173, or 19.2% of actual Partner Gini.

      Actual Partner Gini is computed across source countries after summing imports over all HS6 products: Gini_j(sum_p imports_pj).

      Conservative counterfactual keeps each identified product's total imports and observed supplier set fixed, but gives every observed supplier the same product share: imports_pj = product_total_p / observed_suppliers_p. HS6 999999 remains in actual partner totals but is held fixed in the counterfactual. The remaining Gini is the residual partner concentration after within-product supplier dominance is neutralized.

      Explained share is (actual Partner Gini - counterfactual Partner Gini) / actual Partner Gini. Negative values are allowed. The full-diffusion upper bound spreads every product equally across all active partners in the country-year; it is a deliberately strong benchmark and collapses Partner Gini to zero by construction.

      Dashed 0.5 line: a symmetric random positive share vector across many active items has Gini around 0.5. In Product-Gini charts those items are active products; in these Exercise 4 Partner-Gini charts, read the line as the same rough benchmark across active source partners.

      Interpretation rule: the teal part of the latest-country chart is the amount of aggregate Partner Gini removed by equalizing suppliers within each identified HS6 product while holding HS6 999999 fixed. The gray part is the residual Partner Gini left after that equalization. The dashed 0.5 line marks the symmetric random share-vector benchmark, not perfect equality. The full-diffusion benchmark is shown only as an upper bound because it also equalizes products across partners that were not observed supplying that product.

      Exercise 11: What the Import-Linkage Test Says

      Bottom line: Exercise 11 weakens the broad intermediate-processing claim. The import products that raise total Product-Gini concentration across HS6 products are generally less export-linked, including among intermediates. The stronger evidence is supplier-country concentration, not Product-Gini concentration; this leaves room for a narrower China/electronics/machinery-style supplier-exposure story.

      Exercise 11 Regression Audit Trail

      The table and figures separate Product-Gini concentration from supplier-country concentration. This weakens the broad intermediate-processing claim and points to a narrower supplier-exposure channel.

      1. HS6 product-level result

      Within the same country-year, after controlling for product import share and BEC bin, HS6 products with higher contribution to total import Product Gini tend to have lower export value. The export-value coefficient is -3.527; the export-probability coefficient is -0.151.

      2. Intermediate-channel test

      The direct intermediate test is negative. The intermediate minus non-intermediate slope is -0.550, so concentration-driving intermediate imports are not more export-linked than concentration-driving non-intermediates.

      3. Supplier-country concentration

      This is the more supportive channel. Products that make import sourcing more concentrated by partner country are more export-linked; the Partner-HHI coefficient is 0.075.

      4. HS2 robustness

      Broadening from HS6 products to HS2 chapters does not rescue the intermediate-processing hypothesis. HS2 export value remains negative at -0.216, export probability is 0.000, export share is 0.002, and the intermediate-intensity interaction is -0.007.

      5. Oil/gas/gold/coal exclusion

      Excluding HS4 codes 2701, 2709, 2710, 2711, and 7108 does not overturn the result. These rows are only 0.4% of product rows but 14.5% of import value; after excluding them, the Product-Gini and intermediate coefficients remain negative and Partner-HHI remains positive.
