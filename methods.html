Definitions

  • Active Product Gini: concentration across observed positive HS6 product totals within a country-year-flow; absent or zero-trade products are outside the Gini universe.
  • Active Partner Gini: concentration across observed positive destination or source partner totals within a country-year-flow; absent or zero-trade partners are outside the Gini universe.
  • Active Product-partner cell Gini: concentration across observed positive HS6 product-by-partner cells within a country-year-flow; this is not a zero-inclusive universe measure.
  • Within-product supplier HHI: concentration across supplier countries inside one importer-year-HS6 product.
  • Partner-Gini counterfactual: recomputes Partner Gini after each identified HS6 product is split equally across its observed suppliers; HS6 999999 stays in partner totals but is not treated as a product.
  • Full-diffusion upper bound: recomputes Partner Gini after each HS6 product is split equally across every active partner in the importer-year.
  • Lumpy-product exclusions: HS27 oil/mineral fuels, HS71 precious stones/metals, HS88 aircraft, HS89 ships, and HS93 arms.
  • HS2-preserving benchmark: randomizes within broad HS2 sectors while preserving HS2 totals and active HS6 counts; it is a conditional benchmark, not complete randomization.

Harmonization

HS6 means a six-digit Harmonized System product code. The key problem is that the same six-digit number is not a timeless product identity: the Harmonized System is revised, codes split, codes merge, and reporters adopt revisions at different times.

Why We Harmonize

For a one-year product Gini, a native HS6 code is a useful label. For a time comparison, it can be misleading. If one old code is split into three new codes, a native-code panel can make a classification edit look like product entry. If several old codes merge, it can make product exit or rising concentration appear mechanically.

The estimand for product-level time results is therefore concentration or growth across economically comparable product families, not across whatever native code labels happened to be active in that year.

Where We Do Not Harmonize

Partner-only concentration does not need a product identity after products have been summed into reporter-partner totals. In that case the ranked objects are partner countries, not products, so a value-preserving product remap leaves the partner totals unchanged.

For partner-only measures, HS6 999999 is included in partner totals because it is still trade with a known partner. For product-dependent measures, 999999 is excluded before aggregation because it means Commodities not specified, not a real product.

Product identity rule used for longitudinal product analysis

xH0c,p,t = Σh,r xc,h,r,t × wh,r → p,H0

In plain English, product-dependent longitudinal outputs use the official Harvard Growth Lab / Dataverse weighted conversion tables motivated by Lukaszuk and Torun (2022). Each source revision-code value is multiplied by its conversion weight and then summed into a fixed HS1992/H0 product code.

The source of truth is the weighted classification conversion table DOI 10.7910/DVN/6AADMR, version 2.1. Adjacent HS conversion tables are composed back to HS1992/H0, positive outgoing weights are normalized within source code, and unchanged-code identities are filled where the official adjacent files omit them.

HS6 999999 is excluded before conversion for product-dependent outputs. After conversion, rows are aggregated by reporter, year, flow, partner where relevant, and target HS1992 product; validation checks weight coverage, duplicate target keys, and value conservation.

Why not convert everything to the latest HS revision?

HS1992/H0 is the target because it is the earliest HS vintage in the project window, so every later revision can be converted to the same long-run product universe. Converting the full window to a later revision would require projecting early codes into product splits that did not exist yet.

Coarser HS4 or HS2 groupings are useful robustness checks, but they answer a different question. They measure concentration across headings or chapters, not across HS6-level HS1992 products. Native HS6 counts are kept as a diagnostic because they show how much the raw code universe changes over time.

World-Relative Product Gini

This export-only measure asks whether a country's LT/HGL-weighted HS1992 product-family basket is uneven relative to the product composition of world trade, not merely unequal across the country's own active product codes.

Question

Is the country unusually tilted relative to the world product basket?

Short Answer

A conventional Product Gini treats product codes as equal-sized slots. That is a useful first pass, but it makes a small product category and a massive product category count symmetrically as classification units. The World-Relative Product Gini changes the comparison set. It asks: after giving each product family the weight it has in leave-one-out world exports, how uneven are this country's relative export intensities?

If a country exports every product family in exactly the same proportions as the leave-one-out world basket, every relative intensity equals 1 and the World-Relative Product Gini is 0. The index rises when a country's export basket is concentrated in products that are much larger in that country than in the world benchmark, or when it is thin in large world products.

Why A Product-Code Gini Is Not Enough

The motivating diagnostic is the mismatch between an HS section's share of distinct HS6 export products and its share of export value. If product codes were all similarly important economic slots, the points in the scatter would sit near the 45-degree line. They do not.

In rd2 country exports in 2021, Section 16, machinery and electrical equipment, has 14.5% of distinct HS6 export lines but 28.9% of export value. Section 11, textiles, has 14.8% of lines but only 4.0% of export value. Counting HS6 lines equally therefore gives textiles and machinery roughly equal classification mass, even though they carry very different export-value mass.

That is the reason for using a world-weighted benchmark. A product family that is large in world trade gets a large benchmark weight. A small product family does not become important just because the classification system gives it a separate code.

Formal Construction

Let xcpt be exports by rd2 country c, product family p, and year t. Let Bpt be same-year total benchmark exports of product family p in world_broad. A separate world export basket is constructed for each year t; the 2000 index is benchmarked to the 2000 world product mix, the 2024 index is benchmarked to the 2024 world product mix, and so on. The benchmark is leave-one-out, so country c's own exports are subtracted from the world benchmark before weights are formed.

Main measure

Bpt = Σj in world_broad xjpt   is recomputed separately for each year t.
scpt = xcpt / Σqxcqt
P+ct = {p : Bpt - xcpt > 0}
w-c,pt = (Bpt - xcpt) / Σq in P+ct(Bqt - xcqt)
rcpt = scpt / w-c,pt
WorldRelativeGinict = weighted_gini(rcpt, w-c,pt) over p in P+ct.

The weighted Gini is computed over nonnegative relative intensities with positive benchmark weights. With normalized weights, the conceptual expression is:

Gw(r) = ΣiΣjwiwj|ri - rj| / 2μw,   μw = Σiwiri,   Σiwi = 1.
  1. Compute the country's export share in each product family.
  2. Compute that product family's share in the rest-of-world benchmark.
  3. Divide the first share by the second.
  4. A value of 1 means the country exports the product in exactly the world-normal proportion.
  5. A value above 1 means the country is overweight in that product relative to the benchmark; a value below 1 means it is underweight.
  6. The Gini then summarizes how uneven those relative intensities are, giving larger world products larger weight.

This is Balassa-style revealed comparative advantage logic: compare a product's share in a country export basket to that product's share in the world export basket (Balassa 1965). The index differs in two ways: the benchmark is leave-one-out world_broad exports, so the country itself does not enter its own world basket and large exporters do not get mechanically smaller Ginis from pulling the benchmark toward their own product mix; and the full vector of product-level relative intensities is summarized with a weighted Gini rather than reported as product-by-product RCA values.

Why Weighting Is A Standard Move

Survey-weight analogyWorld shares as weightsA survey-weighted income Gini gives more weight to a sampled household that represents more people. Here, a product family receives more weight when it represents a larger share of leave-one-out world exports.
Economic slots, not code slotsWorld trade massCars, machinery, oil, and electronics get more benchmark mass than tiny product categories. That prevents the HS codebook from becoming the implicit weighting scheme.

Implementation Details

The official website-facing version is export-only, uses the rd2_countries country sample, and is balanced over 2000-2024. The final panel has 55 countries observed in all 25 years, for 1,375 country-years. The all-available rd2 input has 60 countries somewhere in the period, but the balanced panel intentionally drops countries without full 2000-2024 coverage.

Data lineageGuardrails
  1. Start from Comtrade final annual HS bulk files.
  2. Keep export flows and non-world partner rows.
  3. Aggregate to reporter-year-classification-HS6 product export values.
  4. Drop HS6 999999, "Commodities not specified", before product aggregation.
  5. Convert product codes into LT/HGL-weighted HS1992 product families.
  6. Aggregate to reporter-year-product-family exports.
  7. Build same-year world_broad product-family benchmark exports.
  8. For each rd2 country-year, subtract the focal country's exports from the world benchmark.
  9. Compute the weighted Gini of relative intensities.
  • 999999 is blocked in rd2 product aggregates, benchmark aggregates, and generated outputs.
  • The country-product vector is outer-merged with the world benchmark so absent products enter with country share zero.
  • Products with zero leave-one-out world weight are excluded from the ratio and recorded as zero_weight_country_export_share.
  • Negative leave-one-out weights would invalidate the country-year metric; diagnostics report zero such products.
  • The benchmark is world_broad, not rd2 countries reused as their own benchmark.

Contribution diagnostics for small countries

The contribution diagnostic asks what changes most when a product is removed from the weighted-Gini calculation. Because a Gini is nonlinear, these leave-one-out contributions are not additive structural shares. They are still useful for checking whether high values come only from tiny niche products or from large benchmark products.
